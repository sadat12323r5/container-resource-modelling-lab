\chapter*{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Capacity planning determines how computing systems must be provisioned to maintain acceptable performance under load. Although courses such as COMP9334 teach queueing theory, operational laws, and discrete-event simulation (DES), students rarely see how these models relate to the behaviour of real systems. This limits their ability to understand modern challenges such as bursty workloads, saturation effects, and tail-latency escalation that dominate performance in today's distributed environments.

This thesis establishes the foundation for bridging that gap through a lightweight, empirical experimentation framework that runs on student-level hardware. The platform enables controlled performance measurement under varying workloads, providing real traces of latency, utilisation, and service-time behaviour. These measurements support a hybrid modelling approach in which machine-learning techniques learn empirical performance characteristics, while DES incorporates those characteristics to predict system behaviour.

The work in Thesis A develops the motivation, literature context, and initial experiments required to support this framework. Beyond its educational value, the project also targets a contemporary industry problem: predicting latency, saturation thresholds, and tail-behaviour in modern systems using data-driven models. The resulting methodology aims to unify theory and practice, offering students—and practitioners—a modern and realistic approach to capacity planning.
